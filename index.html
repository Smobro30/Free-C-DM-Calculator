Welcome to Dehn's PSW Model

What is Dehn's PSW Model?

Dehn's Pattern of Strengths and Weaknesses (PSW) model is a comprehensive, research-based approach to identifying Specific Learning Disabilities (SLD) through the systematic analysis of psychological processing strengths and weaknesses.

Key Features of Dehn's Model

üß† Comprehensive Coverage

Analyzes 14 distinct psychological/cognitive processes that are essential for academic learning, going beyond traditional CHC theory.

üìä Statistical Rigor

Uses statistically significant intra-individual discrepancies to identify true processing deficits and strengths.

üîó Research-Based Links

Requires documented research-based relationships between processing weaknesses and academic deficits.

üíª Software Support

Utilizes the Psychological Processing Analyzer (PPA) software for accurate statistical analysis.

Why Use Dehn's Model?

  • Enhanced Diagnostic Accuracy: Reduces false positives by requiring both normative and intra-individual weaknesses
  • Neuropsychological Foundation: Based on brain-behavior relationships and cognitive neuroscience
  • Intervention Planning: Identifies specific processing deficits to target with evidence-based interventions
  • Preponderance of Evidence: Aligns with best practices for SLD identification in Idaho and other states

The 14 Psychological Processes

Dehn's model includes 14 distinct psychological/cognitive processes that are highly related to academic learning. These go beyond traditional CHC broad abilities to include critical neuropsychological functions.

1. Attention (AT)

A complex neuropsychological function used to focus on certain stimuli for information processing. Foundation for all higher-order processing. Critical for mathematics skills.

2. Auditory Processing (AP)

The ability to detect and process meaningful nonverbal information in sound; analyze and synthesize auditory information. Essential for reading and language skills.

3. Executive Functions (EF)

Cognitive control and behavioral/emotional control including concept generation, problem solving, attentional shifting, planning, organizing, working memory, and retrieval fluency.

4. Fine Motor (FM)

Precise hand and finger movements required for writing and other academic tasks. Critical for written expression.

5. Fluid Reasoning (FR)

Novel reasoning and problem-solving ability; flexible control of attention to solve unfamiliar problems. Involves manipulating rules, abstracting, generalizing, and identifying logical relationships.

6. Verbal Long-Term Recall (VLTR)

The ability to store, consolidate, and retrieve verbal information over time (minutes to years) through association. Critical for all academic learning.

7. Visual-Spatial Long-Term Recall (VSLTR)

The ability to store and retrieve visual-spatial information over extended periods. Important for mathematics and visual learning.

8. Oral Language (OL)

Linguistic processes involved in verbal communication, including word structure, meaning, sentence relationships, and functional language use. Foundation for literacy.

9. Phonological Processing (PP)

Manipulation of phonemes that comprise words. Includes phonemic awareness, detection, matching, isolation, deletion, segmentation, and blending. Most critical for basic reading skills.

10. Processing Speed (PS)

The ability to perform simple, repetitive cognitive tasks quickly and fluently. Affects fluency in reading and all academic areas. Influences working memory efficiency.

11. Visual-Spatial Processing (VSP)

The ability to perceive, analyze, synthesize, manipulate, and transform visual patterns and images. Important for mathematics and visual problem-solving.

12. Orthographic Processing (OP)

The ability to form, store, and access orthographic representations (written word forms). Critical for reading fluency, spelling, and written expression.

13. Verbal Working Memory (VWM)

Limited capacity to retain and manipulate verbal information simultaneously over short periods. Essential for reading comprehension, math calculation, and written expression.

14. Visual-Spatial Working Memory (VSWM)

Limited capacity to retain and manipulate visual-spatial information. Important for mathematics and visual problem-solving tasks.

Process Clusters

These 14 processes are interrelated and often function in clusters:

  • Working Memory (VWM, VSWM), Attention (AT), Executive Functions (EF), Fluid Reasoning (FR), Verbal Long-Term Recall (VLTR), Processing Speed (PS)
  • Executive Functions (EF), Attention (AT), Verbal Working Memory (VWM), Fluid Reasoning (FR)
  • Oral Language (OL), Auditory Processing (AP), Phonological Processing (PP), Verbal Long-Term Recall (VLTR), Verbal Working Memory (VWM)

Diagnostic Criteria & Decision-Making

Understanding "Deficit" vs. "Weakness"

‚ùå Normative Weakness Only

Definition: Score below 90 (low average or below)

Status: NOT sufficient for SLD diagnosis

Prevalence: ~16-25% of population

‚ö†Ô∏è Intra-Individual Weakness Only

Definition: Significantly lower than person's own mean

Status: NOT sufficient for SLD diagnosis

Prevalence: Nearly everyone has one

‚úÖ DEFICIT (Required for SLD)

Definition: BOTH below average AND intra-individual weakness

Status: Required for SLD diagnosis

Prevalence: Statistically unusual

‚≠ê Asset/Strength

Definition: Average or above AND intra-individual strength

Status: Required to show pattern

Note: Average range can be strength

Cutoff Scores and Statistical Significance

No Universal Cutoffs!

There are NO single, universal scores that definitively indicate strength or weakness. Determination is based on individual's performance across multiple processing domains.

Step-by-Step Workflow

Follow this systematic approach to conduct a comprehensive PSW assessment using Dehn's model.

Referral and Background Review

  • Review referral concerns and academic areas of difficulty
  • Examine student's educational history and previous interventions
  • Document intervention fidelity, frequency, duration, and intensity
  • Review attendance and opportunity to learn
  • Consider exclusionary factors

Plan Assessment Using Process-Academic Correlations

  • Identify the academic area(s) of concern
  • Consult the Process-Academic Correlation table
  • Select processing areas with strongest and strong correlations
  • Choose appropriate tests and subtests using selective cross-battery approach
  • Ensure composite scores (not just subtests) will be available
Example: For Basic Reading Skills difficulties, assess: Phonological Processing (PP), Crystallized Intelligence (Gc), Short-Term Memory (VWM) (Strongest Influence), and Auditory Processing (AP), Long-Term Retrieval (VLTR), Processing Speed (PS) (Strong Influence).

Conduct Comprehensive Assessment

  • Administer standardized academic achievement tests in areas of concern
  • Administer cognitive/processing assessments using multi-battery approach
  • Ensure at least 2 subtests per processing area for composite reliability
  • Include cognitive ability measure (for predictor score)
  • Conduct classroom observations
  • Gather curriculum-based measurement data

Convert Scores to Common Metric

  • Convert all scores to standard scores (Mean = 100, SD = 15)
  • Calculate clinical composites for processes without standardized composites
  • Average subtest scores from same process area
  • Verify score conversions are accurate

Check Subtest Score Unity/Cohesiveness

  • Examine subtests within each processing composite
  • Look for significant scatter (‚â•15 point differences)
  • If significant scatter exists, consider administering additional subtest
  • May need to examine narrow abilities separately
  • Non-unitary composites should be interpreted with caution
Important: If subtest scores within a processing area vary widely, the composite may not validly represent that process.

Identify Normative Strengths and Weaknesses

  • Mark scores ‚â§ 90 as normative weaknesses (W)
  • Mark scores ‚â• 110 as normative strengths (S)
  • Scores 91-109 are average range
  • Record all classifications systematically

Identify Intra-Individual Strengths and Weaknesses

  • Use cognitive composite (e.g., FSIQ, GAI) as predictor OR calculate cross-battery mean
  • Calculate discrepancy: Obtained Score - Predicted Score
  • Apply critical value for significance (typically 12-15 points)
  • Mark significant positive discrepancies as intra-individual strengths
  • Mark significant negative discrepancies as intra-individual weaknesses
Recommended: Use the PPA software or published discrepancy tables that account for reliability coefficients and standard error of measurement.

Identify DEFICITS and ASSETS

  • DEFICIT (D): BOTH normative weakness (‚â§90) AND intra-individual weakness
  • ASSET (A): BOTH normative strength (‚â•110) AND intra-individual strength
  • Note: Average range scores (90-109) can serve as strengths if not intra-individual weaknesses
  • Pattern requires at least one deficit AND at least one strength/asset

Verify Research-Based Relationships

  • Consult the Process-Academic Correlation table below.
  • Verify identified deficits have "Strongest" or "Strong" relationships with academic area of concern
  • If deficit has no research-based link, it cannot support SLD in that area
  • Document the research basis for relationships

Check for Consistency

  • Compare processing deficit score(s) with related academic achievement score
  • Both should be similarly low (consistency)
  • Processing score could be lower than achievement score
  • Significant inconsistency weakens diagnostic case
Example of Consistency: Phonological Processing = 75, Basic Reading Skills = 72 (consistent). Phonological Processing = 75, Basic Reading Skills = 98 (inconsistent).

Consider Interrelated Processes

  • Examine clusters of related processes
  • Consider how deficits in one process may affect others
  • Example: Processing Speed deficits may "pull down" Working Memory
  • Neuroanatomical connections (e.g., prefrontal cortex processes)

Apply Preponderance of Evidence

  • Integrate all data sources (testing, observations, history, interventions)
  • Consider contextual factors (age, validity of assessment, administration issues)
  • Professional expertise and clinical judgment
  • Is it more likely true than not that student has SLD?
  • Document decision-making process transparently

Make Diagnostic Decision

  • Review all diagnostic criteria systematically
  • SLD diagnosis requires: Low achievement + Processing deficit(s) + Pattern + Consistency + Research link + Exclusionary factors ruled out
  • Document how each criterion was met or not met
  • Use "stranger test" - could someone unfamiliar with criteria understand your decision?

Plan Interventions Based on Processing Profile

  • Identify specific processing deficits to target
  • Select evidence-based interventions for identified deficits
  • Leverage processing strengths in instruction
  • Include recommendations for accommodations
  • Develop IEP goals addressing both academic skills and underlying processes

Documentation Requirements

  • Complete analysis table showing all processing scores, discrepancies, and classifications
  • Clear explanation of how deficits were identified
  • Research citations linking processes to academic areas
  • Narrative describing the pattern of strengths and weaknesses
  • Explanation of consistency between processing and achievement
  • Discussion of preponderance of evidence
  • Clear diagnostic statement and rationale

Important Notes

  • All processing areas have SOME relationship with academic skills, but those listed show the strongest correlations.
  • A deficit must have a strong research-based relation to the academic area to support SLD diagnosis
  • Example: Phonological Processing deficit supports Basic Reading SLD but NOT Math Problem Solving SLD
  • When conducting assessment, evaluate processing areas with highest influence regardless of method used.

Psychological Processing Analyzer (PPA) Software

What is the PPA?

The Psychological Processing Analyzer (PPA) is specialized software developed by Milton J. Dehn to facilitate the statistical analysis of cross-battery test scores and assist with SLD identification using the PSW approach.

Key Features

üìä Statistical Analysis

Identifies statistically significant intra-individual strengths and weaknesses among 14 cognitive processes and 8 areas of achievement.

üîó Consistency Checking

Compares cognitive processing weaknesses with closely related areas of deficient achievement automatically.

üìö Multi-Battery Support

Analyzes scores from more than 80 different test batteries for selective, cross-battery testing.

üìù Comprehensive Reports

Generates reports with tables, charts, statistical analyses, narrative, recommended SLD categories, observations, and intervention recommendations.

Alternative to PPA: Manual Analysis

While the PPA greatly facilitates analysis, evaluators can conduct Dehn's PSW analysis manually by:

  • Converting all scores to common metric (SS with M=100, SD=15)
  • Calculating discrepancies from cognitive composite or mean
  • Using published tables for critical values (accounting for reliability)
  • Consulting Process-Academic correlation tables
  • Systematically documenting all analyses

Manual analysis requires more time but follows the same principles as the PPA.
